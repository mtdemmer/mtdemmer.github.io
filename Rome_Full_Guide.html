Our Rome Black Book 2.0

By Yolo Journal · most recent update Oct 2025. Saved here for personal reference — original article and photos at yolojournal.com/rome-black-book.

It’s been nearly four years since we published our first Rome Black Book! Yes, we always make updates when we have them, but since I’ve been a resident here for three years (albeit part-time!) I wanted to rework it, given how much better I’ve come to know the city. Now that it’s full-on summer in the 90s every day and the streets are crowded with tourists, I’m very aware of how tough it can be out there— and I think I’ve figured out some great tools and secrets and other really helpful info to arm you with.

Of course you want to know which restaurants to prioritize, but sometimes you also just need a recommendation near you. You might be across town from that restaurant you’d planned on for lunch, and now you’re desperate for something that is just close and good enough.

I was telling my friend Stephen that my fantasy for Yolo is that it makes you feel the way you do when you’re somewhere unknown, trying to figure it out with no plan—and you see someone in front of you who has good style and looks clever. You find the courage to go up to them and ask them where you should go that’s nearby, and they kindly tell you their favorite place, making you feel like you’ve just hit the jackpot. That’s my personal goal with this Black Book. Usually, I pester all my friends and their friends who know a city super well to give me their intel, but this time I’m taking it all on, with the exception of our food/drinks category! I’ve done so many hotel walkthroughs, talked to so many people, and gone through my 9,468 photos of Rome from these last couple of years, and am sharing everything I’ve learned with you here.

If you’re here in high summer, you may want to reference my Summer in Rome List (it includes easy beach-day trips from the city), and if you’re looking for ideas for everywhere else in Italy, don’t forget about our Italy Travel Planners—we have three!—for the North, Central, and South. —Yolanda

EAT + DRINK

Below are my favorites, and those of a few close local friends. I’ve organized it more emotionally than by location or meal, and I especially wanted to find the best places near the must-sees. For the most part, a restaurant that is good for lunch is also good for dinner. I’m not including the usual suspects like Al Moro, Da Enzo, or Matricianella—not because I don’t like them, but because they appear on many other lists, and honestly, they’re filled with too many people who’ve read about them too. Instead, I’ve focused on places that my Roman friends love, and are still beloved by locals.

In contrast to our other Black Books, I only asked a small handful of friends to weigh in: Marie-Louise Sciò, the brilliant mind behind Issimo and Il Pellicano; Emily FitzRoy, who’s been masterminding the most insidery trips via her Bellini Travel, which turns 25 this year; Andrea Provvidenza, the designer of Lodental; Roberto Polidori, who owns Genco Barbiere, and his artist girlfriend Anna Cerofolini; and Margherita Cardelli of Giuliva Heritage, the chicest Roman atelier.

Safe Haven Spots Near Touristy Locations You’ll Likely Find Yourself In

It’s inevitable and happens to all of us, with best-laid plans gone awry: the line was much longer than anticipated, you woke up too late, your lunch date across town was canceled… and now you’re in the middle of the most touristy area of Rome with no plan. Fear not, I’ve asked my friends to weigh in on this, and below we’re sharing our favorites near the most obvious attractions. (Anything here is also

described more extensively within its own category.)

Pantheon

I’ve just visited La Minerva, which is a block from the Pantheon and has the most beautiful rooftop restaurant. Highly recommend it for lunch, dinner, or drinks! The lobby bar is also beautiful, and would be a perfect spot for a drink on a very hot day. It’s just heaving everywhere within a couple of blocks, but if you just walk even 5 minutes away, there are plenty of great places that don’t cater to tourists. A casual option would be Caffè Portoghesi or Lagana.

Spanish Steps

Depending on how close you want to be, 1956 Frattina Cafe is just two blocks off Piazza di Spagna, but senza tourists! It attracts locals, especially people who work in the area. If it’s lunchtime, I’d try to get into Nino or Settimio al’Arancio, or Hostaria Da’ Pietro. An easy casual lunch spot is the wine bar Buccone, and also consider the lovely Hotel d’Inghilterra for lunch or drinks. If you want to splurge, there’s nothing prettier than sitting in the garden at Hotel de Russie. And Emily says to “hide in the courtyard at Palazzo Doria Pamphilj at the Caffè Doria.”

Piazza Navona

Since it’s so in the middle of many neighborhoods, this has a lot of options. I am so fond of Caffè Perù, which is about a 5-minute walk from here. Closer, quite chic, and open all day is Bar della Pace. The Pantheon is also nearby, so those options apply here as well. My friend Margherita says to “get there at 8am when it’s empty and there’s only the sound of seagulls. Grab coffee and bring a book.” Bob and Anna second the Bar della Pace: “It’s good for an all-day food and drink experience,” and also suggest Libera + Soon. Bob, Anna and Emily all like the Chiostro del Bramante Caffetteria Bistro, which serves food and makes lovely drinks as well, and has a view into the Church of Santa Maria della Pace from the Hall of the Sibyls, where you can look right into the Raphael fresco. Bob recommends the risotto at lunch! Also, da Memmo for excellent pastas!

Colosseum

Personally I love to visit this area at night, especially after dinner at Rocco. It’s also very close to Monti, which has lots of options like Alle Carette for pizza or Er Baretto for coffee. But if you have to be there during the day, Emily suggests Taverna dei Fori Imperiali, “A very good restaurant and still very local.” And Rocco is also open for lunch!

Vatican

The only time I’m in this area is when we go on a run, so I’m relying on my friends for this one! Emily has a dear priest friend who brought her to this great sushi spot called Nakai just minutes from St.

Peter’s, and she said it was fantastic. Roberto loves Cresci, “an all-day-long spot from the oven: early- morning for hot fresh bread, to breakfast, lunch, aperitivo and dinner.” And Anna suggests Romanè.

For gelato, Hedera is excellent.

My Restaurants on Repeat

Rocco; Nino

La Matriciana is one of my favorite restaurants—it’s been around since 1870, and besides being so elegant, the food is just stellar and the owners are so kind. info@lamatriciana.it for reservations.

Rocco – They only make whatever they find at the market in the morning, so it is a small and very thoughtful menu. We aren’t dessert people, but you have to order it here. Excellent wine list and a very local crowd.

Nino is one of the most beautiful dining rooms, with old school waiters (Dario has been working there for 44 years!) in white jackets and black bow ties. I love their ascé burro e salvia–like a burger cooked with butter and sage, the polpette with pureed potatoes, and the veal liver with onions.

Da Cesare al Pellegrino opened a couple of years ago and is a favorite for lunch (primarily because the lighting is so bright at dinner!). An emphasis on local and organic, thoughtful preparations of classics, and interesting wines you’ve never heard of—you’ll be taking pics of the labels so you can find them back home.

Due Ladroni is excellent for fish and sits on a pretty square, so some compare it to Pierluigi. It has less flash and is a bit more intimate.

Lagana for lunch, no matter the season. It has outdoor seating on the street, but if it’s really hot we prefer inside. Let them bring you the endless antipasti (plates of prosciutto, sauteed zucchini, celery with pecorino, so many vegetables, and my favorite–the frittata), and hopefully save room for their pasta pomodoro, which is my favorite in the city.

Ristorante Pietro Valentini is run by Simona and her husband, who is the cook. It’s simple and perfect, and if you’re friendly with her, she’ll be your best friend by the end of the meal. They serve lunch and dinner (closed on Sundays).

Lagana; Bar della Pace

Shiroya is an excellent Japanese restaurant near Campo de Fiori with great sushi as well as cooked dishes. It’s also a favorite of Alessandro Michele, who we have seen there many times.

Caffè Perù is one of our favorite bars for a stand-up drink (near Campo dei Fiori). Besides their fantastic 5-euro Negroni, they make great toast sandwiches—my favorite is the spicy salami. If you want a great sit-down cheap lunch, they make a delicious spaghetti al limone, eggplant parm, and sometimes we just go for a plate of all their vegetables.

Bar della Pace is a new favorite for us. When we’ve made no plans and want something simple like a perfect salad and chicken, we head here. In the summer, we prefer to sit inside its air conditioned and quite chic interior.

La Quercia is a chic little neighborhood spot just off Piazza Farnese. We always have a great meal here, and it seems to still be relatively undiscovered by tourists.

Colline Emiliane is a favorite in the wintertime, for its gnocco fritto and pasta fatto a mano.

Evangelista when it’s artichoke season!

Best Coffee/Breakfast

Pretty much every place that makes a coffee in Rome is going to be good, and a fraction of what you pay for it back home. I’m just listing the places where my friends and I go to on repeat.

Caffe Portoghesi; Giolitti

Vitti for a shakerato (basically espresso over ice shaken and strained) at the bar. It’s 4 euros and it’s a masterpiece! Perfect jet lag fix. Sure, you can sit down at their cafe tables in the piazza, but it’s double the price!

Caffè Portoghesi is our neighborhood spot—you can stand up at the bar, or there is covered outdoor seating. The special thing here is the te freddo, which is a bit like an Arnold Palmer, and I wrote about it here.

Giolitti has great coffee, and it’s surprisingly not crowded in the morning. They also make a great ham/cheese toast and have lots of pastries, if that’s your thing. If you come here for an afternoon coffee, spring for the Mont Blanc Piccolo and thank me later.

La Buvette “In the heart of downtown Rome, excellent tramezzini, croissants and cappuccinos, also

good for a light lunch.” -Andrea

Retro Bottega makes a delicious coffee in a beautiful ceramic cup for when you’re missing a new-school coffee fix—they also make an avocado toast, if you’re missing that too. It attracts mostly locals in the morning, and it’s on a small street where you can sit outside and watch the neighborhood go by. It’s also a favorite of Roberto and Anna: “For us it’s the best, because they have a great selection of high quality coffee, homemade croissants, cinnamon rolls, and cake. Also omelettes, avocado toast, and yogurt with fruit, honey and granola!”

Libera + Soon is a cafe-meets-gallery which attracts the cool Roman crowd, including the most famous designer living in town and his best friend, who is a very famous singer. They serve a perfect espresso in a vintage cup and they also make great salads for lunch. Also a favorite of Roberto and Anna.

Bar San Calisto in Trastevere is no secret, but for good reason: it’s so damn charming, in an opposite of Caffè Greco sort of way (the most gorgeous historic but super expensive cafe in town). The coffee is

cheap; pick up a 12 euro t-shirt to bring home.

Libera + Soon; Retro Bottega

Roscioli Bakery is a favorite of all my friends I quizzed! Margherita says it’s “a real Roman welcome in the city,” and Emily notes “it’s delicious, although very crowded.”

Other suggestions from Emily: “Musee Canova Tadolini is nuts and really fun. And the two cafes on Piazza del Popolo are quite good, Canova and Rosati–you choose which to head to depending on your politics: left wing goes to Rosati and right to Canova.”

Caffè Perù if you want to start the day with locals – Margherita Le Levain, a favorite of Roberto and Anna, “is a bit of France in Rome—excellent croissants, pain au chocolat, quiche and the best madeleine in town. They just opened a new one on Via Piave, where you can sit down. Also, Iginio Massari is maybe the best pastry chef in Italy—his maritozzo and Christmas panettone are amazing.”

Sunday Lunch

Pierluigi

Al Pompiere: When it moved from the second floor to street level, we were worried it would lose something—we loved the light in its previous space. But it is just as great, and even easier to get a table since they have so much more room. We always get the baccala and a pasta, and the bottle of house white that is 11 euros! Go for Sunday lunch when there are big tables of local families and the vibe is so great!

Pierluigi is a place that attracts Roman families for Sunday lunch year-round, and is great for people watching. “Pierluigi hands down,” says Margherita.

La Terrazza, the rooftop bar and restaurant at the Bulgari Hotel, has an epic Sunday brunch, which is an endless buffet, with the most beautiful food, and endless pouring of Ruinart Blanc de Blanc. The people watching is almost like inside La Grande Bellezza.

Checco Er Carettiere is a favorite of Emily’s. “It’s great for families—really fun and bustling and noisy and delicious with a great gelateria next door for pudding afterwards.” (It’s also open 7 days a week for

lunch and dinner.)

Ristorante Tiberino: I’ve been here for Sunday lunch in the winter, and it was so sunny, glorious, and delicious. Andrea loves it as well: “The gourmet den on Isola Tiberina, a stone’s throw from Trastevere. Loooooove this place, especially for lunch overlooking one of the prettiest squares of Rome.Trattoria tables with white tablecloths, efficient and friendly service, everything is good and seasonal…in the heart of Rome, but you don’t feel like you’re in Rome.”

Sunday Dinner/Monday (when so many places are closed)

La Campana

La Matriciana dal 1870, which I wrote about in my “on repeat” section above, is closed on Saturdays, but open on Sundays.

La Campana – The oldest (500 years) restaurant in the city is so solid—the pastas, polpette, the veal breast with potatoes, the puntarelle…and one of the best desserts I’ve ever had—a bitter orange tart with dark chocolate gelato.

Fiammetta – My Roman friends come here for the pizza, but I think the pastas are great, too.

Da Cesare al Pellegrino – I wrote about it in my “on repeat” section—it’s a favorite everyday!

Al Vero Girarrosto Toscano – If you’re into steak tartare and want a break from pasta, a Roman friend of mine loves this spot for their tableside tartare.

È Passata La Moretta on Via di Monserrato might be a spot you’d walk right by, but the food is excellent and it attracts a very local crowd.

Da Teo is just around the corner from Da Enzo, which always has a line so long we rarely go. This place is incredible, takes reservations, and we have also been able to just walk in. Try the seppie e carciofi (cuttlefish with artichokes), carciofo alla Romana, and puntarelle—and their pastas are all great.

Da Francesco, best known for its pizzas, is an easy place for families and a group, with several floors and outside area, and a full menu beyond pizza.

Il Corallo next door to Da Francesco, also has great pizza and pastas, and is apparently where all the Gucci creatives like to go.

Settimio All’Arancio is open Mondays (not Sundays) and has a great selection of fish, but also meat, if there are others in your party who want something else! I love the insalata di baccalà (cod salad) and tonnarelli spigola e bottarga (sea bass and cured fish roe).

Tullio (Mondays but not Sundays) has an incredible wood-fire oven, and their meats are so good– especially the lamb. But all of their dishes are great.

Hostaria da Pietro – Besides the excellent food, I love the interiors here, and we are always amused by the very animated owner. Don’t sleep on their desserts. (Open Mon-Sat)

Pierluigi is the most fun restaurant in town, and you go there for fish. In the summer, everyone sits out on Piazza de’Ricci, and it’s a great people-watching situation. Cocktail Hour

Obviously aperitivo hour is big here, yet after-dinner drinks aren’t! Even in the small cafes around the city you can order a cocktail, although it’s more in the Aperol spritz world–you go to a proper bar for something more complicated. It isn’t a big bar town, but here are our favorites, from piazzas to rooftops.

Best Hotel Bars

La Minerva; Hotel Locarno

Hotel Locarno is one of the prettiest spots in town to have a drink–nothing better than sitting in their garden courtyard.

The Bulgari rooftop bar is divine! And Emily agrees: “The Bulgari rooftop has the best view, at sunset when the swallows are out—so elegant. And the square (Piazza Augusto Imperatore) is finally finished and just incredible.”

Orient Express La Minerva just opened a rooftop restaurant and bar which has the most epic views over the Pantheon, as well as a lobby bar that is super sexy with low light.

The iconic 150 year-old Hotel d’Inghilterra is a favorite, especially in the colder months, when we love to get a drink at the cozy Caffè Romano Lounge Bar. In warmer months the rooftop bar is lovely.

Grand Hotel Plaza has a great interior bar (Mongiardino designed), as well as terrace bars. It’s completely caught in time–you come here for the untouched interiors, not for the crowd, because there isn’t one!

Hotel Mediterraneo for a drink at their amazingly preserved Art Deco bar (the one inside, not the rooftop–but that is nice too).

Hotel de Russie’s Stravinskij Bar in their legendary garden is a treat!

Best Aperitivo

Vitti; Camponeschi

Ciampini and Vitti are both great places for aperitivo. I prefer Vitti, because they have homemade potato chips.

Caffe Portoghesi for simple aperitivo—the snacks they serve are delicious, and they are super friendly.

Camponeschi on Piazza Farnese for its Roman crowd, its view onto the square, and excellent drinks!

They’re so friendly, even if there’s a big security guy at the front. Be prepared to sit inside if it’s crowded, or wait for a table.

Caffè Peru is a favorite for simple, inexpensive drinks and a great vibe! Most people stand around outside and it’s quite a scene.

L’Oratorio Trastevere is a seasonal bar (April-late Oct) that is outdoor in a courtyard of a church. It’s definitely a spritz or Negroni kind of place—don’t come here and try to order a martini or anything too complicated.

Wine Bars

L’Angolo Divino near Campo dei Fiori is a charming wine bar with a thoughtful edit.

Enoteca Buccone is just near Piazza del Popolo, yet still feels super local. An excellent list of wines and champagnes by the glass, along with delicious snacks to go with.

Achilli al Parlamento may be known for its Michelin-star restaurant in the back of its extensive wine shop, but I prefer the front outdoor cafe for aperitivo, simple lunch, and their excellent wine list and wines by the glass.

Farmers’ and Food Markets

Testaccio; Piazza delle Coppelle

There is a sweet little farmers market in Piazza delle Coppelle every morning, and the Monte d’Oro market is lovely, also for a coffee and takeaway food. Crowded but still lovely (as long as you get there early, is Campo dei Fiori, (daily except Sunday). If you’re here on a Saturday, there’s a great organic market by Circo Massimo called Campagna Amica Market. The Testaccio market is a great place to meander and cobble together different snacks that add up to lunch. There are so many more markets than this–so if this is what you’re after, do more digging to find what suits you.

Our favorite place to pick up prepared foods is at Angelo Feroci, and for organic groceries, there are Naturasi markets around the city.

WHERE TO STAY

While I don’t check into many hotels here since I have my own apartment, I definitely visit many of them, since hotels in Rome are so much a part of the culture. All my local friends love to have lunch or drinks at the Bulgari, the de Russie, and now at La Minerva. And I rely on word of mouth from people I trust who come here at least once a year. There’s been such a hotel boom here in the last couple of years with newcomers (Six Senses, Casa Monti, Palazzo Talia, Bulgari, Palazzo Ripetta), and the Corinthia is opening just across from the Parliament building at the end of the year. We’re listing our favorites in categories that range from splurge to cheap and cheerful, design-y to old school, and best outdoor pools thrown in for good measure.

Splurge

Hotel de Russie

The Bulgari – If there is one hotel to splurge on in this town, for me, this is it. For the architecture, the pool, the rooftop bar in the summer, and the cafe all year round on street level—but also just the kindest staff and a great location across from Augustus Mausoleum, which recently completed a restoration and reopened to the public. (You can read our full Guest Book here.) Hotel Vilon is where I’ve stayed over the last few years, and my friends who stayed here recently said it’s still great.

La Minerva is the first hotel from Orient Express, which opened this Spring in a 17th-century palace near the Pantheon. The interiors are really beautiful and warm, with lots of terracotta, ochre and amber incorporated throughout. We’ve only heard great things so far!

Hotel de Russie – The garden is so divine, as is the service, with a great location by the Spanish Steps.

The decor isn’t exactly my taste, but most of the people I know who love it just overlook it because it’s so great in all the other ways.

Rome Cavalieri – This ‘60s-era hotel is brilliant, but not for everyone, given that it’s a 10-minute ride to Piazza del Popolo from its location on the highest point of the city. I recommend it for visitors who are not on their first trip to the city (or who’ve very clearly said they don’t want to be traipsing about in the heat), and for families. It feels more like a resort than a city hotel—it has 15 acres, a beautiful outdoor pool that is 11 feet deep (for all of you who love diving into a pool), excellent poolside lunch, and a great spa (first time I’ve seen a cold plunge inside a steam room–genius). Decor is a mashup of brutalist architecture (it was Conrad Hilton’s 2nd hotel project) and an incredible collection of Italian and French art and antiquities of the owners who bought it in the ‘70s. Don’t miss the view from the 9th floor over the entire city.

Palazzo Ripetta – A five-star that has rooms under 1K a night is sadly a deal in summer these days.

There’s a Pomodoro sculpture in the entrance, and a very big interior garden with lots of seating—but it does feel quite new. I thought the interior bar seemed cozy and not overdesigned, as many bars can be.

Palazzo Shedir – Set in a Palace that was the former home of the Borghese Family, this might be as close as you can get to sleeping in a museum.

Palazzo di Pietre – Not quite a hotel (no bar or restaurant), more like luxury apartments with services: a front desk, a beautiful little gym, breakfast, and incredible rooms—huge, beautifully and thoughtfully appointed, with kitchenettes and art from the owner’s personal collection. The location is excellent on a tiny little street five minutes from the Pantheon and Piazza Navona. (Read the Guest Book here.) Palazzo Roma – Seemingly every wall is frescoed and everything that could be marble is, in this former palace.

J.K. Place Roma – A favorite of many–it feels more like your home in Rome than a hotel with a grand entrance and public spaces. It’s definitely a more intimate option, but it’s still on the spendier side.

St. Regis Rome – Originally opened by hotelier Cesar Ritz in 1894, it is very historic and underwent a thoughtful renovation a few years ago. They invested a lot in the arts and even have their own orchestra!

Palazzo Manfredi – You’re so close to the Colosseum, you could practically hit it with a misfired Prosecco cork from the balcony of your room.

Hotel Eden – Interiors are really airy and elegant, with huge windows in the rooms and restaurant that have great views of the city. The location right by the Borghese gardens couldn’t be better, and we’ve heard a rumor that they can arrange special access to Galleria Borghese.

Hotel de la Ville – Rooms are tastefully modern, there’s a subterranean spa with thermal baths, and the colorful Cafe Ginori is definitely a highlight, with everything plated on the signature porcelain.

Fendi Private Suites – If you’ve ever dreamed of sleeping in a store, you kind of can—the third floor of Palazzo Fendi has seven suites.

Six Senses Rome – The first urban hotel from Six Senses feels more contemporary than Roman, but it has an impressive spa on par with their other properties, a lovely rooftop, and great service. (Read the

full Guest Book here.)

Hotel d’Inghilterra – This intimately scaled hotel, which was once a hub for British aristos on their Grand Tour, just underwent a major renovation involving acres of sumptuous and colorful silks. It has a sexy little lobby bar and a great location on a quiet side street just a minute from the Spanish Steps.

(Read the full Guest Book here.)

Old World

Grand Hotel Plaza; Hotel Locarno

Residenza Ruspoli Bonaparte – Beautiful historic rooms within a palazzo in the center of it all—it isn’t a full service hotel, there isn’t 24/7 room service or a bar, but you’ll feel like you’re a Roman aristo, and you have molto options once you walk out the front door.

Grand Hotel Plaza has the most divine entrance and public spaces, all designed by Mongiardino. Sadly the guest rooms are not so interesting, but the main area is one of the most beautiful rooms you could have a drink in, albeit a lonely one, since you’re probably the only people there. In the summer the rooftop terrace is open.

Hotel Locarno is definitely everyone’s favorite hotel, although many mourn their recent price increases. It’s so soulful, and they’ve kept all the beautiful historical details in the rooms, plus there’s the interior courtyard for a drink—it just doesn’t get better.

Villa Spalletti Trivelli is a favorite of many of our friends. Old-school luxury, family owned. In between Monti and the historic center.

Donna Camilla Savelli – A former monastery in Trastevere, it has a lot of character and we love the walks up to Gianicolo or Villa Pamphili from there.

Boutique Hotel Campo de’Fiori for completely caught-in-time rooms with lots of damask wall coverings and velvet headboards—don’t go changing!

On the More Design-y Side

G Rough; Casa Monti

Casa Monti: A new and very chic hotel in the Monti neighborhood that embraces the modern, rather than living in the past that surrounds it. Locals come here for drinks and dinner, and it feels young and

alive, which is refreshing! (Read our full guest book here)

Hotel de Ricci is just off of Via di Monserrato, in one of my favorite neighborhoods. It’s so charming, with lovely and super comfortable rooms and great staff/service. And a very full continental breakfast.

Martius Private Suites is just next to Giolitti inside a nondescript courtyard. Our friends who stay there swore us to secrecy on it (but you’ll keep this between us, right?).

The Hoxton – It’s not necessarily in the heart of the center, but if you love running, it’s a great choice, because it’s right above the Borghese Gardens.

Palazzo Talia – With parts of the former palazzo-turned-school-turned hotel designed by Luca Guadagnino, it would be wrong to not include this recent opening. There are elements I like, but on the whole, stylistically it just wasn’t my vibe and I’d rather stay a bit further away from the chaotic Trevi Fountain.

Palazzo Ripetta – The rooms don’t look over-designed, and we love their use of color and the big Murano glass chandeliers.

Hotel Adriano – For their 60th anniversary this year, they did some renovations and opened a new terrace with great views.

The Edition – This Ian Schrager-designed hotel is definitely geared towards more modern tastes—the rooms have lots of light wood, slabs of muted tone marble, and big windows framed with wispy curtains.

G Rough – Art enthusiast and 7th-generation Roman, Gabriel Salini, designed this 10-room hotel to feel like your own pied-a-terre. The MO here, and at their sister hotel in Puglia, Palazzo Daniele, is “questa casa non è un albergo,” or, “this house is not a hotel.”

Hotel dei Barbieri – While it looks quite modern, they worked with artisans to keep many of the original features of this 17th-century palace, like the beautiful wooden ceilings, marble columns and frescoed archways.

Rhinoceros Roma, designed by Alda Fendi, has a gallery, restaurant, and interesting apartment-ish rooms, near the Forum and Circo Massimo.

Cheap and Cheerful

Hotel Mediterraneo

Hotel Mediterraneo – If you appreciate modernist architecture and don’t mind being in the train station neighborhood, this completely caught-in-time and not renovated (in a great way) hotel has a great rooftop and rooms for less than 200 euros a night.

Residenza B – A reader reports: “A gorgeous 4 bedroom apartment which has been designed by high profile architect Stefano Dorata. It is in a brilliant location and looks onto the Barberini Gardens.”

Mama Shelter is in the Prati neighborhood, which I like because it isn’t so touristy, but it is quite deep in it, so not a place to stay if you want to roll out of bed and feel like you’re in the center of town.

Hotel Lunetta – Small but updated option with a rooftop and a spa Tree Charme Parlament is a 23-room boutique hotel within two palazzetti. The neighborhood and the street are great–next to one of the best wine shops/restaurants, hairdresser, and natural grocery store in town, and across the way from Tebro, who make the best linen pajamas.

Otium has a rooftop bar with great views, and is well located near Piazza Venezia.

Hotel Nazionale may not have charming interiors, but it’s on the most elegant square with no cars, and barely a tourist—and simultaneously a quick walk to everything you’d want to visit.

Albergo del Senato is smack in front of the Pantheon, which means by early morning and late night you’ll love your location, when the crowds have dispersed.

Hotel Astrid is in Flaminio—a quieter, more residential neighborhood with grand buildings—a quick bike or tram ride to Piazza del Popolo. A Roman friend of mine in the design world always stays here– it’s always less than 200/night, and it’s nice enough.

Hotel Sant’Angelo is in Prati, just a block from the river, so five minutes walk to the very center. I haven’t stayed here, but I did a walk through and it looked charming enough!

With an Outdoor Pool (good for families!)

Rome Cavalieri adult pool left and kids pool right

Rome Cavalieri: This Waldorf Astoria-owned hotel has the most impressive pool setup in town, with a centerpiece pool that goes to 11 feet deep! There’s also a really big children’s pool and an indoor pool for the winter. Plus, in the summer, there’s a kids club. It is a 10-minute drive to the Piazza del Popolo, so it is best if you’re planning to see the city in the morning, come back here for lunch and a poolside afternoon, and then return later in the day to the center.

Villa Agrippina Gran Melia, right by the Vatican, is probably the most resort-like hotel in the center of the city. You actually feel like you have space, and the pool is great. The design is a little much in the rooms, but if you’re traveling with a family, you’ll forget about it, glad that you can spend the hottest part of the day at the pool and walk to dinner in the center.

Palazzo Dama has a lovely pool, albeit not as big as some, but it’s so close to Piazza del Popolo and Villa Borghese. The bigger the pool, the further out from the center!

Mercure Roma Centro Colosseo – While I’m the last person to recommend a chain hotel, this literally has a rooftop pool with a view to the Colosseum.

Grand Hotel del Gianicolo – Not in the center of things, but it is wonderfully old-school and has a big outdoor pool.

Anantara Palazzo Naiadi has a rooftop pool and outdoor lounge where parents can have a cocktail while kids splash around.

Soho House – Reviews are mixed on this Soho House–it’s in the San Lorenzo area, which is where our mechanic is, to give you a sense. If you prioritize a great gym and rooftop pool and have been to the city so many times you want to experience something a bit more local feeling, I’d say it’s great for that.

Hotel Villa Pamphili has an Olympic size pool and is in the Villa Pamphili park area. Yes, it’s a 15-minute drive to Trastevere, but it’s almost like being in the countryside.

Rentals

Casa Tre Sarte

Casa Tre Sarte is a new discovery for me—really tastefully designed rooms in the Monti area, and you can also rent the whole house. It’s owned by a couple who make really lovely clothing that they sell in their store on the same piazza as Pierluigi.

Il Mulino di Trastevere – Well-designed apartment-style rooms, great neighborhood, and they work with a service called My Extra Home, which will supply your kitchen from Eataly and book babysitters, laundry service, etc.

CasaCau private apartments (no elevator)

The Luxury Travel Book – Many apartments that are one bedrooms, and some bigger–but what I like about this curation is many of the listings have old school interiors, or no art on the walls. I particularly like Apartment Palazzo Borghese, Via Giulia, and Via della Vitrina.

One Fine Stay has interesting options for a larger family/group, although a lot of them have some strange modern furniture and art choices. Definitely on the higher end—I particularly like the Piazza Mattei, which has Renaissance frescoed ceilings, albeit some weird modern art, too.

My Extra Home has a lot of rentals on their site as well—some look better than others. Given that they have smart services available, I’d definitely recommend opting for this over an Airbnb.

SHOPPING

Rome isn’t a city that you come to specifically for the shopping, but it’s something you may weave into the other things you are there for. So I’m kicking this off with shopping neighborhoods/streets, and what is nearby—so the history buff, vintage seeker, and food focused in the group can all be happy.

Flea market finds; Via di Campo Marzio

Around the Colosseum/Roman Forum

The Monti neighborhood is right next door, which has a lot of good vintage and antiques shops. I love this area at night—if we are eating dinner at Rocco, I make sure we are walking to or from dinner, so we can see ancient Rome along the way.

Around Piazza del Popolo

Via dell’ Oca is just nearby and has a number of beautiful independent stores, like Atelier Bomba and Laura Urbinati (in case you forgot a swimsuit!), as well as a hat maker and a beautiful florist. There’s also Hotel Locarno, which is perfect for a cocktail, or a shakerato pick-me-up! Don’t miss the Caravaggio in Santa Maria del Popolo.

Around Piazza di Spagna

Obviously there are the main shopping streets of Via Condotti and Via del Babuino, but don’t neglect the side streets, which have smaller brands and less crowded (and less expensive) food/drink options.

My favorite restaurant in the neighborhood is Nino, and I also love Bar Frattina, which attracts the locals who work in the area.

Around the Pantheon

Just a few blocks from the Pantheon is Via di Campo Marzio, which has Davide Cenci, Borsalino, Slowear, Momoni, and a number of other independent shops. Tebro is on Via de Prefetti, as is De Clerq & Clerq, and then down the next street, on Via Metastasio, there are several shops to wander into like Pratesi, which restores and sells classic handbags, and Booktique, one of our favorites. On Via della Scrofa there are several nice shops, like Furia Living (a well curated lifestyle store) and Grifoni (elevated basics for men and women). Stop at Caffè Portoghesi for a coffee or a panino, and if you want to have a proper sit-down lunch, the local favorite is Lagana, where they’ll bring you so much antipasti you’ll be so full—but definitely get whatever pasta they suggest and split it. Don’t miss the Caravaggios in San Luigi dei Francesi and the Basilica of Sant’Agostino.

Around Piazza Navona

Via del Governo Vecchio has a lot of vintage shops and is just a couple of blocks away, Via Parione has galleries, small stores and cafes, and all the Pantheon suggestions above work for this area as well. Bar della Pace is a great destination cafe/bar/restaurant that is open all day, and while Via dei Coronari can feel a bit congested given its narrowness, it’s worth walking down (Lisa Corti, Massimo Alba). My favorite museum, Palazzo Altemps, is a block off the piazza, never is crowded, and its ticket is good for Palazzo Massimo and the Crypta Balbi as well.

Around Campo de’ Fiori

Yes, this food and flower market is filled with tourists, but come early before everyone else does, and it’s magical. You’re in Roscioli territory, so you can head there for breakfast afterwards, or Caffè Perù.

There’s great shopping on Via de Monserrato (Chez Dede, Le Tre Sarte, Labsolue), Via del Pellegrino (Giuncart, Non Solo Oro) and some interesting stores on Via dei Giubbonari, and a couple of good shops on Via dei Chiavari. Museum-wise, Galleria Spada and Palazzo Farnese are right here… although you definitely need to reserve Palazzo Farnese well in advance.

Souvenirs

Piazza Borghese; Emporio Centrale

Since my job is to tell you about places you haven’t read about everywhere else, I’m not going to wax on about Schostal the way I did in our last editions on Rome. ICYMI, it’s a Roman institution where locals have always gone to get their basics, from t-shirts to underwear to socks to…pajamas, which is what everyone who isn’t a local now goes there for. Somehow, Schostal pajamas have become the cool Rome souvenir! (I’ll say it’s because we wrote about it, but I think Harry Styles being quite vocal about it might be more the case). I hear its success means it can get picked over with very few sizes left—but you should still stop in and check it out and say hi to Shirley, one of the kindest people in Rome. My latest discovery for pajamas is Tebro, a 6th-gen family-owned store that has the most incredible linen pajamas, for the same price as the ones in cotton at Schostal. It’s not a place you go for hugs and community (like Schostal is!), but it has beautiful robes, smoking jackets, and anything in the bedding world you could imagine.

Emporio Centrale is hands-down my favorite place to pick up Italian souvenirs. I wrote about it here, and a year later, I’m just as excited about it. Trust me–if Rome is your last stop, you could literally pick up all the small souvenir gifts you want to bring back here, and not spend time looking elsewhere. From the corno (the red pepper talisman) to Venetian slippers, they’ve curated the very best and also surprising made-in-Italy objects.

Gammarelli for the long Pope or cardinal socks–for both adults and little ones.

La Bottega del Marmoraro is one of the most charming ateliers you can visit in the city, where a second-generation marble carver makes custom signs. It’s the best spot to pick up presents for friends, although they’ll weigh down your bags!

Giolitti might be everyone’s favorite gelato spot, but I prefer the mornings when it’s filled with friendly locals having their morning cappuccino. Bring it home with you by getting one of their espresso cups and saucers for 10 euros, or the cappuccino set for 12.

C.u.c.i.n.a is a kitchen store right near the Spanish Steps, that has these fantastic metal tins that say biscotti, cafe, te, sale….and we always pick one up as a gift for Matt’s mom.

Giuncart has been making baskets, hats, boxes—you name it—with straw, since 1960. A hat or a small purse would make a nice/flat gift to bring back.

Ibiz Roma is a 50-year-old family business that makes leather accessories—from a dog collar and leash combo to a beautiful woven wine bottle holder for your bicycle.

Booktique – While it’s primarily a well-curated book shop, they also have Venetian slippers and cute tote bags with Roman expressions like “Daje” (come on!).

c.u.c.i.n.a.; Emporio Centrale

Fabindia Roma – An Italian home, clothing and accessories store, with beautiful block prints made in India. My favorite gifts from here are their oven mitts, toiletry bags and notebooks, and a beautiful indigo bedspread for me.

Antica Cartotecnica – A very old store that sells deadstock notebooks, typewriters, pens, inkwells, and is right near the Pantheon.

Dante Mortet’s atelier is where this 5th-generation sculptor and chiseler makes bronzes of hands, feet…

primarily for celebrities, using the same ancient techniques as his predecessors.

Bar San Calisto has the best t-shirt to bring back—a superb logo.

The priest calendar is something you’ll see in every tourist kosk–and I just think it’s a quirky gift for the right person.

Fashion

Chez Dédé; Atelier Bomba

Atelier Bomba has lovely off-the-rack, beautifully made classics (especially the delicate knitwear!), but if you want a dress or suit custom-made, this is the spot. Prepare to spend some time here because Cristina and Michele (mother and son) who own it, are the most charming, warm hosts.

Giuliva Heritage is a beautiful clothing line for men and women, based in Rome. They don’t have a store here (yet!), but they have a gorgeous atelier on Via di Monserrato. You can make an appointment if you’re a serious shopper by contacting them on their site.

Chez Dédé always has something interesting–from Viennese pajamas and nightshirts (great summer dresses), to their bags, jewelry, antiques, and books. Pair it with a visit to Caffè Perù, or lunch at Pierluigi, also on Via di Monserrato, which always has a new store opening up, and stop in at Maison Halaby (beautiful handbags), and at L’Archivio di Monserrato, Soledad Twombly’s store.

Alla Giulia makes the cutest Venetian-style slipper shoes, but with fringe. Also, you can get your initials customized. The slippers for little kids are troppo cute.

Calzature Campo Marzio has cute velvet house slipper slides that are very chic and perfect for a dinner party or walking around a hotel (streets and rain would ruin them), and also Mary Jane Venetian slippers that I haven’t seen anywhere else!

MaxMara and Weekend MaxMara: Why would I recommend a global brand here? Because the pricing here is so much better than anywhere outside of Italy, plus you get the tax refund. I bought a coat for 1,800 euros that was $4,000 in the US, plus I got the tax back.

Aspesi – This is an Italian brand I adore. They make the best pants and shirts—elevated basics. The pants have the best cut–a wide leg that is slim on the upper thigh-so they are actually flattering. And they have a forgiving elastic waistband that is wide and doesn’t cut into you. They always produce this pant—in a crisp cotton in summer, and a nice wool for the colder seasons.

Lisa Corti is a great place to pick up a perfect summer dress.

Davide Cenci has an excellent edit of clothing and accessories for men, women, and kids.

Laura Urbinati for Italian swimsuits that are divino!

Mostly for Men

Matt wrote about his men’s favorites here, which is mainly clothing, but also some cigar intel.

Battistoni is just off Via Condotti in a quiet courtyard that opened in 1946 doing custom clothing.

Today it’s more of a multi-brand store with some of the usual suspects, but always with some Italian brands I didn’t know about. It also has a great art collection from famous friends who decorated it with their paintings and drawings.

Massimo Alba, Slowear, and Aspesi all have beautifully made knitwear and good seasonal basics.

Brugnoli is a fantastic men’s store near the Vatican, which focuses on shoes, like Alden and Paraboot, but also clothes and other accessories.

Italy has several brands that do well priced and very smart looking menswear–think on the fly, inexpensive, the kind of place you want to go if your luggage doesn’t arrive! Matt likes Doppelganger, Boggi Milano, and Spada Roma, all of which have several locations in town. The cuts are classic, and you can find long-sleeved linen shirts, polos and well fitted Bermuda shorts. Also, cotton or linen blazers in good colors and classic Italian style for under $500.

Vintage Clothing

There’s great vintage shopping in the Monti area—our favorites are Pifebo Vintage, King Size Vintage, Flamingo Vintage. If you don’t mind picking through, Humana (they also have several shops around town) often has great stuff (Matt has scored a lot of vintage tennis kits here).

Via Governo Vecchio has some great vintage—Cinzia, Ciao Vintage, Omero + Cecilia—to name a few.

Best to wander around and discover.

Mercantile has a big selection of men’s and women’s clothing, in a spacious storefront.

Twice – A favorite for clothes, accessories, and jewelry in Trastevere.

31 Vintage Avenue near Campo de’ Fiori always has great Gucci and Ferragamo bags, as well as workwear and dress up, in pristine condition.

Home/Antiques

Molto Collectibles

Dispensabile is one of the coolest home stores I’ve been to, period. It seems like an architect’s office with a small retail area, but then you go upstairs and it’s one room after another, each styled in such an inspiring way with all the products they sell. They don’t sell online, but they do have a registry section, which makes me want to renew our vows.

Decorarte Flli Pinci for the best handmade brass home details—everything from doorbells and handles to lighting fixtures.

Stay makes the most beautiful bedding and table decor pieces. Love their linen sheets and waffle cotton blankets, as well as their great palette for napkins and tablecloths. All made in Italy.

Vittorio Bagagli is a homeware store that’s been around since 1855. It’s the kind of place that fancy Romans would have their registry, and it’s a good spot to pick up some special Italian glassware.

There is a whole exciting world of lampshades in Rome. Especially the half shade, which is such a revelation and I don’t know why it isn’t common in the States. Favorite lamp shops are Il Paralume on Via di Monserrato, Paralumi, and I Paralumi di Lorenzo Senzacqua.

Antonella Petraglia has a great edit of antiques from the turn of the 20th to mid-century.

Industria Garage Roma has an incredible selection of Thonet.

Molto Collectibles is a fantastic edit of mostly 20th century furniture, in a very interesting location in the Prati area.

Antica Libreria Cascianelli – Definitely a bric-a-brac vibe in here—from rare books and maps to vintage postcards and photographs—this is allegedly where Alessandro Michele (former Gucci creative director) likes to linger.

Flea Markets

Porta Portese; Piazza Borghese

Piazza Borghese has lovely framed drawings and some antiques—it’s worth a gander.

Ponte Milvio has a flea market on Sunday along the river.

Porta Portese is where you’ll always find me on a Sunday morning. Begin at Piazza Ippolito Nievo– don’t walk or get dropped off at Porta Portese. Trust me—if you do, you’ll think I’ve sent you to a bad flea market with lots of tube socks and new-fangled plastic kitchen wares. I’ve been going every Sunday when I’m in Rome for over three years, and I think you’ll like my game plan. Look for the seller with lots of linens and vintage clothing hanging on the left side if you’re facing the market—she is the first seller on the left. She has great linens if you’re looking, by the way. Hug that left side path, and then at the bottom of the road, stay left until it comes to the end, then make the right to loop back. Once you’ve covered that side of the loop, you’ll come up to the top, and now take the next road—which, with your back to the market and your face to the street, will be to your hard left. We usually go down about two solid blocks and then make the loop back. There’s something for everyone here–Matt likes vintage glass ashtrays, I obviously gravitate to vintage travel ephemera. Our daughter likes the clothing. It isn’t great for furniture. People will say you have to go super early–if it’s the winter, I say go before 10, if it’s the summer, go before 8:30—more because it gets so hot and crowded by 10:30.

Bathrooms aren’t so great—there are some cafes along the way, but think truck stop. We tend to bargain, and you do have to have some grasp of Italian if you’re going to do that, so at least learn your numbers, and “quanto costa?” If someone has a low price on something, I don’t bother asking for a better price. I do dress down when I go there—it is not a place to wear an outfit you’d then wear to a nice lunch afterwards. Or a nice watch. I take a canvas bag for putting my stuff inside, and I hold it tight, and I’ve never had a problem. My best score? A Gucci letter holder in green for 15 euros.

EXPLORATIONS + INTEL

This is a walking city! There are so many gems you’ll miss if you just taxi around, but it does require the right (albeit most likely not the chicest in your suitcase) footwear, and being strategic about the time of day that you’re on the streets. (I’m talking summer here–any other time of year you don’t have to worry about the oppressive heat.) Also, those taxis are often in short supply (or worse, on strike), and ancient cities weren’t built for cars—so it can end up taking just as long to drive as to walk. When you walk, you are always passing by churches or small galleries that you can just duck into to find the most incredible Raphael or photography exhibit. I’m not going to list all of the museums and other sites you could see here, as other sources do that much better. Instead, I’ve pulled together some of my favorite resources and guides, as well as a handful of uncrowded places I always steer friends to, and tips and hacks for families, drivers, and gifts for your host!

Under the Radar Galleries and Museums

Palazzo Altemps

Obviously, there are a million cultural stops you’ll be making, and we’re not listing our suggestions here because there are too many, plus other people do that better—but our favorite museums are Palazzo Altemps (one ticket gets you access Palazzo Massimo and Crypta Balbi) and the Doria Pamphilj.

Galleria Spada is also quiet and lovely. My one piece of advice–if you want to go to the Galleria Borghese, book your tickets well in advance!

Alex, who is a huge fan of house museums, suggests the Mario Praz Museum, home of the 20th-century literary critic who wrote about aesthetic taste and Romanticism, is full of Neoclassical furniture, portraits, wax miniatures, and other refined miscellany, and makes for a highly atmospheric visit if you love seeing how people live surrounded by their collections. And the Giorgio de Chirico House Museum, just off Piazza di Spagna, is an amazing glimpse into the private world of the artist, with a collection of his paintings and sculptures as well as objects that reflect how he lived, including his TV.

Churches with Caravaggio Paintings

Chiesa di San Luigi dei Francesi: the Saint Matthew cycle, including “The Calling of Saint Matthew,” “Saint Matthew and the Angel,” and “The Martyrdom of Saint Matthew”

Basilica of Sant’Agostino: “Virgin of the Pilgrims”

Santa Maria del Popolo: “The Conversion of Saint Paul” and “The Crucifixion of Saint Peter”

Villa Pamphili; Fontana dell’Acqua Paola

Walks/Runs

The Villa Borghese Gardens are fantastic for a morning run. (And our favorite coffee spot there is Casina del Lago.) Then again, so is a run to Fontana dell’Acqua Paola. (And a coffee stop at Bar San Calisto!) Further afield is Villa Pamphili, a beautiful park that attracts mostly locals walking their dogs.

A loop along the river is also lovely. You can also do an ancient Rome walk by following the Appia Antica, which will bring you past the Catacombs of Saint Callistus and San Sebastiano.

Villa Borghese; Piazza Navona

Parks/Playgrounds

Rome isn’t strong on playgrounds. Our friend with small children heads to the Villa Borghese for that, or to Piazza Navona, where there are no cars. Another friend suggests Villa Balestra, which has basketball courts; Castel Sant’Angelo, which has a playground area; and Piazza San Cosimato in Trastevere, which has a jungle gym and swingset.

Barbers/Hairdressers

Genco Barbiere, the oldest barber shop in town, is owned by our dear friend Roberto, who does all of the shaves. It’s in a beautiful building (there’s a gorgeous garden terrace above it that is owned by a certain famous designer) and it’s where everyone from politicians to kids come to get a haircut or a shave. Tell him I sent you!

Roberto d’Antonio has a salon on Via de Prefetti, as well as in the Bulgari Hotel. It’s definitely the fancy option in town if you need to get a cut or color.

Mani-Pedi

Nailbar on Piazza Rondanini or Via Giulia

Massage

Bulgari Hotel – I’ve had the best massages of my life here. And then you can use the beautiful pool afterwards.

Hacks/Tactics

Whatever you can get tickets for in advance, do. Time the indoor visits during the heat of the day, and do anything else in the morning or evening. Bring a reusable water bottle with you when you’re out for the day, because you can refill it along the way at any of the many drinking fountains you’ll see all over the city.

Taxis

Uber works here–and it’s excellent for anything you need to reserve in advance. It can be much more expensive than taxis though–a taxi is 55 euros from the airport, and an Uber can be even 30 euros more.

Download the Wetaxi or It Taxi App before you arrive.The local taxis are fast, and most take credit cards, even if they don’t really want to.
